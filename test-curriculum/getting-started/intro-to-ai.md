# Introduction to AI for Digital Science Staff

## What is AI?

Artificial intelligence refers to systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. This includes recognising patterns, understanding language, generating text, writing code, and making decisions based on context. Modern AI tools used in workplaces - like large language models - are trained on vast amounts of text and learn to predict useful outputs given a prompt.

For Digital Science staff, AI is not a replacement for expertise. It is a force multiplier. A researcher who knows their domain can use AI to work faster, explore more ideas, and produce higher-quality first drafts. The value comes from the combination of your knowledge and the model's breadth.

## Key Concepts

**Large language models (LLMs)** are the foundation of most text AI tools today. They predict the next token (roughly a word) based on context. This means they are excellent at generating plausible, coherent text - but they can also hallucinate facts that sound correct but are not.

**Context window** is how much text the model can "see" at once. Modern models can hold tens of thousands of words in context, which means you can paste in a long document and ask questions about it.

**Temperature** controls how creative vs. deterministic the output is. At low temperature the model picks the most likely continuation; at high temperature it takes more creative risks. Most productivity use cases work well at low temperature.

**System prompts** are instructions given to the model before the conversation starts. Forge uses system prompts to give the AI knowledge about your organisation and adjust its behaviour.

## Getting Started

Here are the key concepts every Digital Science employee should understand before using Forge or any AI tool at work:

1. AI outputs require verification - especially facts, numbers, citations, and code. The model may be confidently wrong.
2. Prompt quality drives output quality. A vague question gets a vague answer.
3. Iteration is normal. Refine your prompt, ask follow-up questions, or ask the model to revise.
4. Context matters. The more relevant background you provide, the better the result.
5. AI is not magic. It does not have real-time access to information (unless explicitly connected), does not remember previous conversations by default, and does not understand your business the way a colleague does.

## Practical Tips

Start with prompting - learn to write clear, specific prompts before trying advanced features. A good prompt states the goal, the audience, any constraints, and the desired format.

Use AI as a brainstorming partner. Ask it to generate ten alternatives, poke holes in your argument, or suggest approaches you have not considered. This is one of the highest-value use cases.

Always verify AI outputs. For internal documents this might just be a quick read-through. For customer-facing content or anything with numbers, check sources.

Paste in context liberally. If you are asking about a specific report or document, paste the relevant sections in. The model cannot read your mind or your hard drive.

Ask for reasoning. Adding "explain your reasoning" or "think step by step" often improves the quality of answers, especially for analytical tasks.

## Common Pitfalls

The biggest mistake new users make is treating AI output as done work. It is a starting point. Review, edit, and apply your own judgment before using anything the model produces.

The second most common mistake is giving up too quickly when the first response is not quite right. Refine the prompt, add constraints, or ask the model to try again differently.

Finally, do not use AI for tasks that require genuine confidentiality guarantees. Understand your organisation's data handling policies before pasting sensitive information into any tool.
