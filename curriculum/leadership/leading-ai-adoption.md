# Leading AI Adoption

## Context

This guide is for managers and leaders at every level of Digital Science. You are the multiplier (or the bottleneck) for AI adoption in your teams. If you engage with AI Tuesdays actively, your team will follow. If you treat it as a distraction from "real work," your team will pick up on that signal.

The Manager's Guide lays out your core responsibilities. This guide goes deeper into the practical leadership work of making AI Tuesdays succeed.

## Your Role: Facilitator, Not Expert

A common misconception is that managers need to be AI experts before they can lead their teams through AI Tuesdays. This is wrong. Your role is not to teach AI tools. It is to:

* **Create the conditions for learning.** Clear the calendar. Protect the time. Make it safe to experiment and fail.
* **Remove blockers.** If someone cannot access a tool, help them get access. If someone does not know where to start, help them pick one task. If someone is overwhelmed, help them simplify.
* **Set direction.** Help your team identify which use cases matter most for your function. Not every AI experiment is equally valuable.
* **Model the behavior.** Share your own learning. Try AI tools yourself. Be open about what you do not know.

You do NOT need to:
* Know how to use every AI tool
* Have all the answers about AI
* Be further along than your team members
* Create curriculum content (that is available through this program)

## Week-by-Week Manager Checklist

### Weeks 1-2: Launch

* [ ] Remove all Tuesday meetings from team calendars for 12 weeks
* [ ] Have a 1:1 with each team member about their starting AI skill level and learning goals
* [ ] Identify the top 3 use cases your function should focus on (refer to the department analysis)
* [ ] Ensure everyone has access to at least one AI tool (Gemini at minimum)
* [ ] Set up a team-specific sharing mechanism (Slack channel, meeting slot, shared doc)
* [ ] Try an AI tool yourself and share what you learned

### Weeks 3-4: Build Momentum

* [ ] Check in: Is everyone actually spending time on AI Tuesdays, or are they doing BAU?
* [ ] Facilitate a team sharing session: what has everyone tried? What worked?
* [ ] Identify anyone who is struggling and connect them with support (accountability partner, specific resources)
* [ ] Share a success story from your team in #AI-Tuesdays
* [ ] Review the use cases: are they still the right ones?

### Weeks 5-6: Deepen

* [ ] Move from exploration to focused experimentation: each person should have one use case they are working on seriously
* [ ] Connect your team with other departments working on related problems
* [ ] Start thinking about Phase 2 (Build and Impact): which experiments should become projects?
* [ ] Check sentiment: are people engaged, anxious, bored, frustrated? Adjust accordingly.

### Weeks 7-8: Phase 2 Kickoff

* [ ] Help your team define their "Build" projects: specific workflows or tools they will create
* [ ] Ensure projects are scoped realistically for 4-5 remaining weeks
* [ ] Connect team members with technical support if they need it
* [ ] Begin documenting time savings and results for the final showcase

### Weeks 9-10: Build and Refine

* [ ] Regular check-ins on project progress
* [ ] Help unblock anyone who is stuck
* [ ] Start thinking about how these AI workflows persist after Week 12

### Weeks 11-12: Showcase and Sustain

* [ ] Each team member prepares to present their results
* [ ] Celebrate concrete wins: time saved, quality improved, new capabilities unlocked
* [ ] Document which AI tools and workflows will become permanent parts of your team's processes
* [ ] Set expectations for AI use going forward (it does not stop after Week 12)

## Handling Common Manager Challenges

### "My team cannot take a whole day off from work"

This is real for customer-facing teams with SLA obligations. Practical solutions from the Manager's Guide:

* Shorten, consolidate, or cancel non-essential meetings across the WHOLE week, not just Tuesdays, to create capacity
* Consider whether select weeks allow a "Limited Service" Tuesday to set external expectations
* Talk with your functional leader about how to balance customer needs with participation
* Remember: the point is not to stop working. The point is to use the day to work on improving HOW you work.

### "Some team members are resistant"

The A.A.A. Framework:

* **Acknowledge:** "I hear you. Our current roadmap is ambitious, and taking a day for learning feels like it adds pressure."
* **Audit:** "Let's look at your Tuesday tasks. Which are repetitive or robotic? That is exactly where we will start our AI experimentation."
* **Align:** "The goal of this refounding is to ensure that in six months, those same tasks take half the time, protecting you from future burnout."

If resistance persists after genuine engagement with A.A.A., reach out to your People Partner. Sustained disengagement may signal deeper issues (role anxiety, burnout, or concerns that need individual attention).

Key principle: "Friction is not a sign of a difficult employee; it is often a sign of a high-performer who is protective of their output and their team's commitments." Treat resistance as information, not insubordination.

### "I do not know enough about AI to help my team"

You do not need to be the expert. You need to:
1. Try one AI tool yourself (start with Gemini in your email or docs)
2. Share what you learned, including what did not work
3. Connect team members who know more with those who know less
4. Ask your team to teach you; this shows vulnerability and encourages participation

### "How do I know if my team is making progress?"

Progress during AI Tuesdays is not about test scores or certifications. Look for:

* **Behavioral change:** Are people using AI tools in their regular work, not just on Tuesdays?
* **Conversation shift:** Are AI topics coming up naturally in team meetings?
* **Time savings:** Can anyone point to a specific task that is faster now?
* **Use case development:** By Week 6, does each person have at least one use case they are working on?
* **Sharing:** Are team members teaching each other without being asked?

In 1:1s, ask these questions:
* "What have you tried with AI this week?"
* "What is working? What is frustrating?"
* "Is there anything blocking you from experimenting?"
* "Do you feel like you are learning and growing, or is this not clicking yet?"

## Rewards and Recognition

The program includes awards and incentives for engagement and collaboration. As a manager, you play a key role:

* **Nominate team members** who go above and beyond, especially those who help others learn
* **Showcase wins** in team meetings. Even small wins matter early in the program.
* **Celebrate learning, not just success.** Someone who tried something ambitious and failed learned more than someone who played it safe.

## Functional Curriculum Development

Your functional leader needs your input to shape the curriculum for your department. Specific ways to contribute:

* Share which use cases your team has identified as most promising
* Report on what tools your team needs and whether they have access
* Suggest how learning should be shared (end of each Tuesday, biweekly, in team meetings)
* Define what success looks like for your team by Week 12

## Wellbeing

From the Manager's Guide: "Exploring AI and new technologies can be exciting but it can also feel mentally demanding." Watch for signs that someone is overwhelmed:

* They seem stressed or anxious about AI Tuesdays
* They have stopped participating or are going through the motions
* They express fear about their job security

If you notice these signs, have an informal conversation. A coffee and a low-pressure check-in is usually more effective than a formal meeting. If concerns persist, connect with your People Partner or the wellbeing resources available through Danielle Feger, the Global Health and Wellbeing Manager.

## Try This Today

1. Block 30 minutes to try an AI tool on one of your own tasks (email drafting, meeting prep, or data analysis)
2. Note what happened: Was it faster? Better? Worse? What surprised you?
3. Share your experience with your team, including the parts that did not work
4. Ask each team member (in person, on Slack, or in a brief team huddle): "What is the one task you would most like AI to help with?"
5. Use their answers to prioritize your team's AI Tuesday focus areas

The most effective thing you can do as a manager is demonstrate that you are learning alongside your team. You are not asking them to do something you are not willing to do yourself.
