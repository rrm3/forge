# Customer Communications with AI

## Context

Every day, members of the Customer department write emails, prepare meeting summaries, draft reports, and create presentations for customers. These communications need to be accurate, professional, and tailored to the audience. AI does not replace the expertise that makes these communications valuable, but it can dramatically reduce the time spent on drafting, formatting, and polishing.

This guide covers practical workflows for using AI in the kinds of written communication the Customer team produces daily.

## Email Drafting

**The 3-step workflow:**

1. **Capture your intent.** Before opening an AI tool, write down (even as rough bullet points) what you want to say, who the audience is, and what action you want the recipient to take. This is the hardest step and the one only a human can do well.

2. **Generate the draft.** Paste your bullets into an AI tool with context:
"Draft a professional email to a university research office administrator. I need to:
* Confirm their Symplectic Elements implementation kickoff date of April 15
* Explain that we need their SSO configuration details by April 1
* Offer to schedule a 30-minute technical call to walk through the requirements
* Thank them for their partnership
Keep the tone professional and warm but concise. Under 200 words."

3. **Review and personalize.** AI gives you structure and polish. You add the personal touches: the reference to your last conversation, the specific detail about their environment, the empathy about their timeline constraints. This is where your expertise shows.

## Meeting Summaries and Action Items

Meeting summaries are one of the highest-value AI use cases because they save time for everyone involved and ensure nothing gets lost.

**If you have a transcript (from Google Meet with "Take Notes for Me" or Gong):**
1. Open the transcript
2. Ask AI: "Summarize this meeting in three sections: Key Decisions Made, Action Items (with owner and deadline for each), and Open Questions. Keep each section to 3-5 bullet points."
3. Review for accuracy. AI sometimes attributes action items to the wrong person or misunderstands technical details.
4. Share with attendees for confirmation

**If you have your own notes:**
1. Paste your raw notes (even messy, shorthand notes work)
2. Ask AI: "Turn these meeting notes into a clean summary. Organize by topic. Extract action items as a separate list."
3. Review and send

**For recurring customer meetings:** Create a prompt template that matches your standard meeting summary format. Over time, your summaries will be faster and more consistent.

## Report Generation

The Customer team produces several types of reports: implementation status reports, quarterly business reviews, research analytics reports for funders, and internal customer health reports.

**Implementation status reports:**
1. Gather your status data (milestones completed, issues outstanding, next steps)
2. Ask AI: "Write a one-page implementation status report for [customer name]'s Figshare deployment. Status: 3 of 5 milestones completed. Current milestone: data migration (70% complete, expected completion April 10). Blockers: waiting for customer to provide remaining metadata mappings. Next steps: complete data migration, begin user acceptance testing. Tone: professional, reassuring, clear about next steps."
3. Review for accuracy, add specific details AI could not know

**Quarterly business reviews:**
AI can help structure and draft QBR presentations:
* "Based on these usage metrics for [customer]'s Dimensions subscription, draft talking points for a QBR. Highlight: overall adoption trends, most-used features, underutilized features we should promote, and recommendations for maximizing their investment."
* "Write the narrative for a QBR slide titled 'Impact Summary' based on these numbers: [paste metrics]."

**Research analytics reports:**
For the research analytics team producing reports for government and funder customers:
* "Draft an executive summary of this grant portfolio analysis. The audience is a program officer who needs to justify continued funding. Focus on research impact, collaboration patterns, and translation to practice."
* Always verify statistics and citations. Funders expect precision, and any factual error undermines credibility.

## Proposal and SOW Generation

Technical consultants and implementation managers frequently write proposals and statements of work. AI can help with the repetitive structural elements while you focus on the solution design.

**Workflow:**
1. Outline the scope: what problem are we solving, what will we deliver, what are the milestones
2. Ask AI: "Draft a Statement of Work for a Symplectic Elements implementation at a large research university. Scope includes: SSO integration, data migration from their current system, user training (3 sessions of 2 hours), and 90 days of post-launch support. Use professional language suitable for a procurement process."
3. Review thoroughly. SOWs become contractual documents. AI can structure and polish, but every deliverable, timeline, and assumption needs human verification.

**For RFP responses:** AI is particularly helpful for the boilerplate sections of RFP responses (company background, security posture, compliance certifications). Draft these once with AI and maintain them as templates. Use AI to tailor the templates to each specific RFP's language and requirements.

## Translation and Localization

Digital Science serves customers globally. AI can assist with translation for customer communications, though professional translation should be used for published or contractual content.

* "Translate this customer email into Spanish. Maintain the professional tone and technical terminology."
* "I received this email in German. Translate it to English and summarize the main request."
* "Adapt this product announcement for a Japanese academic audience. Consider cultural norms around formality and directness."

For quick internal understanding of foreign-language communications, AI translation is usually good enough. For customer-facing responses, have a native speaker review the output.

## Presentation Preparation

**For pre-sales demos:**
* "I am presenting ReadCube to a pharmaceutical company's R&D department. Create an outline for a 30-minute demo that focuses on literature management for drug discovery researchers. Include 3 discovery questions to ask early in the demo."

**For customer training sessions:**
* "Create an agenda for a 2-hour Overleaf training session for a university's graduate students. Include hands-on exercises for: creating a new document, using templates, real-time collaboration, and bibliography management."

**For internal presentations:**
* "Turn these customer success metrics into a presentation outline for our monthly team meeting. Include: key wins this month, challenges, and areas needing attention."

## Building Your Prompt Library

The most efficient communicators on the team will be those who build a personal library of prompts tailored to their specific product area and customer base.

**Start with these templates:**
* One for support ticket responses (per product)
* One for meeting summaries
* One for status report drafting
* One for QBR talking points
* One for proposal outlines

Save these somewhere accessible (a Google Doc, a note in your project management tool, or a saved Gem in Gemini). Refine them each time you use them.

## Try This Today

1. Find an email you sent to a customer this week that took more than 10 minutes to write
2. Write down your intent as 3-5 bullet points
3. Ask AI to draft the email based on those bullet points, specifying the audience and tone
4. Compare the AI draft to what you actually sent
5. Time how long the AI-assisted process takes vs. your original approach
6. Calculate the time saved and multiply by how many similar emails you send per week

The Customer department's first AI Tuesday activity involves exactly this exercise: bring a real (anonymized) customer email or support ticket and practice the AI-assisted workflow. The goal is to quantify the time savings on your most common communication tasks.
