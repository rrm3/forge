# Change Management for AI Adoption

## Context

The People team is not just adopting AI for its own work. It is helping the entire organization navigate the shift to an AI-native way of working. With Ash McDonagh as the AI Capability Lead embedded in the team, the People function is the connective tissue between the AI Tuesdays initiative and the 685 people across Digital Science who are going through this change.

This guide is about the human side of AI adoption: the resistance, anxiety, excitement, and practical challenges that come when you ask an entire organization to fundamentally change how it works.

## Understanding Resistance

The Manager's Guide introduces the A.A.A. Framework (Acknowledge, Audit, Align) for handling pushback. Here is the deeper context behind why people resist AI adoption and how to work with that resistance productively.

### Common Sources of Resistance

**Role anxiety:** "Will AI replace my job?" This is the most fundamental fear, and it is not irrational. The honest answer is that AI will change roles, not eliminate people, but only if people develop AI skills. AI Tuesdays is the investment in making sure everyone is on the right side of that shift.

**Tool overwhelm:** "There are too many AI tools and I do not know where to start." Digital Science has made Gemini, Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, and more available. For someone who has barely used ChatGPT, this is genuinely overwhelming. The fix is to start with one tool on one task, not try everything at once.

**Competence threat:** "I have been doing this job well for years. Now I feel like a beginner." Experienced professionals who are excellent at their current workflows may feel their expertise is devalued. The reframe: their domain expertise is what makes AI useful. AI without their judgment is just fast noise.

**Skepticism about quality:** "AI output is not good enough for my work." Sometimes this is true, and that is good feedback. Sometimes it reflects unrealistic expectations (expecting AI to produce final-quality output on the first try) or limited experience with prompting techniques.

**Workload concerns:** "I do not have time for this. My actual work is not going away." This is validated by the program design: AI Tuesdays explicitly deprioritize other work. But some teams, especially customer-facing ones with SLA obligations, genuinely struggle to take a full day. The Manager's Guide offers practical solutions for this.

### Productive Responses to Each

**For role anxiety:**
Share concrete examples of how AI augments rather than replaces specific roles at DS. A support specialist who uses AI to draft responses handles more tickets with higher quality, becoming more valuable, not less. An engineer who uses Copilot ships faster and spends more time on architecture and design.

**For tool overwhelm:**
Simplify the starting point. Everyone should start with one tool: Gemini (because it is already in their Google Workspace). Master one use case before adding tools. The Tools Overview guide provides a structured way to expand.

**For competence threat:**
Emphasize that AI is a multiplier of existing expertise, not a replacement. The people who will get the most from AI tools are the ones who already know their domain deeply. A finance person who understands reconciliation will use AI for reconciliation far more effectively than someone who does not.

**For quality skepticism:**
Invite them to do a side-by-side comparison. Take a real task they do well, try it with AI, and compare. They will quickly see both what AI does well and where their expertise is essential. This builds appropriate trust rather than blind faith or blind rejection.

**For workload concerns:**
Work with the manager to identify specific tasks that can be deprioritized during the 12-week period. The Getting Started Guide explicitly states: "We are giving you the green light to deprioritize other tasks or projects."

## Creating Psychological Safety

AI Tuesdays requires experimentation. Experimentation requires the safety to fail publicly.

**What psychological safety looks like in practice:**
* Managers share their own AI failures, not just successes. "I tried to use Claude to draft a board report and the first version was terrible. Here is what I learned about prompting."
* Mistakes with AI tools are treated as learning opportunities, never as performance issues. During the 12-week program, people are "not being judged on their standard output."
* Questions like "What is a prompt?" or "How do I use this?" are welcomed, not mocked. The skill survey shows that many employees are at the Basic level. That is the starting point, not a problem.
* Showcases include "what I tried and it did not work" alongside "what worked great."

**Warning signs of low psychological safety:**
* Team members only share polished successes, never struggles
* People quietly stop attending AI Tuesday activities
* Only the already-technical team members participate
* Questions dry up in team sessions and on Slack

If you see these signs, talk to the manager. It usually signals that the team environment needs adjustment, not that the individuals are disengaged.

## The Role of Accountability Partners

The AI Tuesdays program includes AI Accountability Partners: pairs of people (ideally at different skill levels and from different departments) who support each other's learning.

**What good partnerships look like:**
* Weekly check-in: "What did you try this Tuesday? What worked? What did you struggle with?"
* Skill sharing: The more advanced partner teaches a technique; the less advanced partner shares a use case the expert had not considered
* Gentle accountability: "Last week you said you would try using AI for meeting summaries. Did you? What happened?"

**How the People team can support partnerships:**
* Help match partners across departments and skill levels
* Provide a simple structure for check-ins (3 questions, 15 minutes)
* Celebrate partnership success stories
* Re-match partners mid-program if pairings are not working

## Measuring the Human Side of Adoption

The Success Measures document defines three categories: Engagement, Habit Formation, and Capability Uplift. The People team can track the human dimensions behind these numbers.

**Qualitative indicators to monitor:**
* Are people talking about AI in contexts other than AI Tuesdays? (Natural integration)
* Are teams spontaneously sharing tips and wins? (Peer learning)
* Are managers mentioning AI in 1:1s and team meetings? (Leadership reinforcement)
* Are people expressing confidence vs. anxiety about AI? (Emotional readiness)

**Gathering this data:**
* Pulse survey questions: "I feel confident in my ability to use AI tools in my work" (1-5 scale)
* Informal conversations: People Partners can ask about AI adoption in their regular check-ins
* Slack activity: Monitor engagement levels in #AI-Tuesdays
* Showcase participation: Track who presents and who attends

## Supporting Managers

Managers are the multiplier or bottleneck of AI adoption. If a manager is engaged, their team will be engaged. If a manager deprioritizes AI Tuesdays, their team will follow.

**How the People team supports managers:**
* Provide coaching for managers who are struggling with their own AI learning curve (they cannot facilitate what they do not understand)
* Help managers use the A.A.A. Framework with specific team members who are resistant
* Connect managers to each other so they can share what is working
* Flag to leadership when structural barriers (workload, tooling access, unclear priorities) are preventing team adoption

**When resistance crosses into performance:**
The Manager's Guide states: "Friction is not a sign of a difficult employee; it is often a sign of a high-performer who is protective of their output and their team's commitments." Start with support, not discipline. The performance management process is a last resort for sustained disengagement, not a first response to normal adjustment difficulty.

## Long-Term Culture Shift

AI Tuesdays is 12 weeks. The cultural shift it aims to create is permanent. The People team's role extends beyond the program.

**What success looks like after Week 12:**
* AI tools are part of everyday workflows, not a special Tuesday activity
* New hires are onboarded with AI fluency as an expectation
* Job descriptions include AI-relevant skills where appropriate
* Performance conversations include "how are you using AI to improve your work?"
* The organization continues to learn and adapt as AI tools evolve

**What the People team does to sustain the shift:**
* Integrate AI literacy into onboarding for all new hires
* Include AI capability questions in relevant job descriptions
* Support ongoing communities of practice around AI
* Track AI adoption metrics beyond the 12-week program
* Advocate for continued investment in AI tools and learning time

## Try This Today

1. Talk to 3 people outside the People team about how they feel about AI Tuesdays
2. Listen for: excitement, anxiety, skepticism, confusion, or disengagement
3. For each person, identify which type of resistance (if any) they are experiencing
4. Practice using the A.A.A. Framework: Acknowledge their concern, Audit their workload for a good AI starting point, Align the initiative with their personal goals
5. Document your observations and share them with the People team

This exercise builds the empathy and coaching skills that will be essential as the People team supports the entire organization through this change.
